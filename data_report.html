Data Set: Project Gutenberg Frequency and Sentiment Analysis

Project Gutenberg is an online library of books whose U.S. copyright has expired. I conducted a word frequency and sentiment analysis on the fiction for which plain text files are available in the library. Note that the analysis, along with its results are detailed below.

About the Dataset

About 8000 works of fiction with about 2500 different authors were taken from the library and used for analysis. Though Gutenberg does not provide the original publish date of books in their metadata, they do provide the author birth and death year. To gain an understanding of the factors of the analysis over time, birth year was used to order the books in a chronological manor. Figure 1 shows the books used in the analysis, organized by author birth year. Note that if you hover over elements in the graph, annotations will appear.

Figure 1: histogram of plaintext fiction available in the Gutenberg library

Each individual author was hand-tagged with binary gender and birth country. Based on these taggings, 62.1% or 1384 of the authors in the dataset were male, 25.6% or 571 were female, and we were unable to find enough information to declare the gender of 12.% or 274 of the authors.

Because the data set was solely books in English, the authors were largely English or American. We further subdivided authors from the UK into respective states.

When conducting the analysis, I looped over each pertinent entry in the Gutenberg catalog, which can be found here. I constructed two possible urls for the book's plaintext version on the Gutenberg website. I queried the url, scraped all text from the page, stripped the headers, and conducted the analysis. Of the 8362 books in my original set, I was able to successfully complete the analysis for each.

Frequency Analysis

In order to gain a better understanding of the presence of female vs. male characters in the books, I conducted two sets of words: female-specific nouns and male-specific nouns. During the analysis, I identified all sentences containing one or more of the "female-specific" nouns as a "female" sentence. I did the same with the male word set. I returned the number of "female" sentences divided by the number of total sentences as a female frequency value, abbreviated in the resulting dataset as "f_freq". I repeated for the "male" sentences, returning "m_freq". Mostly, the results were unsurprising.
